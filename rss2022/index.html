Description

The key question that we would like to discuss at this workshop is: what should the testing and validation infrastructure for multi-robot and collaborative autonomy look like.

Recent years have seen significant progress made in standardizing robot software, thanks to ROS and its associated simulation tools such as Gazebo. This has enabled researchers to build on each other’s work without having to reinvent the wheel. Nevertheless, there is a critical lack of infrastructure when it comes to multi-robot systems and collaborative autonomy. 


Researchers who are inventing and enhancing new collaboration algorithms sometimes resort to testing their innovations on a small set of simplified scenarios that they have used in their previous work in order to reduce the time and cost of testing. Such testing may be merely a demonstration that the collaboration algorithm works; it may not yield comparisons with existing algorithms that generate insights into the performance and cost tradeoffs; and it may not consider a broad enough set of missions and scenarios needed for test and evaluation, validation and verification (TEVV). The multi-robot systems community needs an infrastructure that supports system development and testing from concept to realization in multiple domains. The goal of this workshop is to bring together researchers and practitioners to understand the infrastructure needs of collaborative autonomy testing. 


The University of Maryland recently received a planning grant from the CISE Community Research Infrastructure (CCRI) program to envision what an Infrastructure for Collaborative Autonomy Testing (ICAT) would look like. Through this workshop, we seek to foster a discussion with the broader community, understand the needs of the stakeholders, and identify effective resources that might be incorporated into ICAT. Through this workshop, a larger portion of the CISE MRS research community will be able to participate in the design of ICAT.


Plans to Encourage Participation

The workshop will consist of invited talks, contributions in the form of position papers, and a panel discussion. We have invited speakers spanning a variety of research areas (researchers who design MRS and collaborative autonomy algorithms, who design hardware testbeds and software tools, and stakeholders) to bring a diversity of perspective for the discussion. We will invite more stakeholders from the industry to participate in the panel discussion. The call for contributions will solicit input from the broader community about their position and/or ongoing research related to building infrastructure for MRS and collaborative autonomy and testing.


In the panel discussion, we will open the floor for a discussion of topics based on the outcomes of a pilot survey sent out to the community. Last month, the organizers had shared a survey to gather information from the broader community (via robotics-worldwide and other mailing lists) about existing multi-robot systems test and evaluation practices, use cases, missing infrastructure, and the needs of the community. We will share the results of this survey with the participants of the workshop, prior to the workshop, so as to structure the panel discussion. We will also solicit additional inputs from the participants based on the results of this pilot survey in order to more concretely identify what a multi-robot and collaborative autonomy testbed looks like. 


Discussion Topics 

The specific goals of the workshop will be to:

  • Discuss the current infrastructure for multi-robot and collaborative autonomy test and evaluation and identify the limitations;

  • Brainstorm about hardware and software system architecture for ICAT and determine use cases;

  • Identify existing technologies that can be used as part of the ICAT; determine their strengths and identify opportunities for improvement.

Physical Resources 

We expect ~50 participants in-person for the workshop. Ability to livestream over Zoom is required (Webcam, microphones, internet connectivity).


Hybrid Format

We plan to make the workshop open to virtual participants. We will use the Zoom webinar for the remote option (or, if available and required, the platform chosen by RSS for the workshop). Participants will be able to register for the workshop to receive the link to join the webinar. The webinar allows participants to view the livestream and participate in the discussion. One of the organizers will always monitor the chat and moderate the remote questions/comments. The talks will all be recorded (with permission) and posted on a YouTube channel for later viewing.
